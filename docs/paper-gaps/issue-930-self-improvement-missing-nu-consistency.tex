\section{The assertion}

The self-improvement step in \cite[Section~9]{Ji2020LowIndividualDegree}
takes a projective measurement $G$, a threshold parameter $m$, a field
size $q$, and a degree bound $d$, together with a parent measurement $A$
(which is a projective $m'$-qubit measurement for some $m'\le m$). The
paper's self-improvement theorem then assumes the following
consistency hypothesis on $G$: on average over $\bu\in\F_q^{\,m}$,
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:paper-nu-consistency}
  A_a^{\bu}\ot I \simeq_{\nu} I \ot G_{[g(\bu)=a]}.
\end{equation}
Here $\simeq_{\nu}$ is the paper's approximation relation comparing two
families of measurements by the expectation of their difference: for a
bipartite state $\ket\psi$, the expectation difference is at most $\nu$.
The parameter $\zeta$ is set to
$\zeta = 3000\,m\,(\eps^{1/32}+\delta^{1/32}+(d/q)^{1/32})$.
Under \eqref{eq:paper-nu-consistency} the theorem concludes completeness
$\bra\psi H\ot I\ket\psi \ge (1-\nu)-\zeta$, point consistency of $H$,
strong self-consistency of $H$ at level $\zeta$, and the operator bound
$\bra\psi Z\ot(I-H)\ket\psi \le \zeta$ where $Z$ dominates the averaged
$A$-operators.\footnote{The paper passages are
\path{references/ldt-paper/self_improvement.tex}, lines~635--671, and the
helper lemma lines~348--433; the induction-section wrapper is in
\path{references/ldt-paper/inductive_step.tex}, lines~149--196. This note
is part of the retrospective audit in \ghissue{930}. The discrepancy is
tracked for resolution in \ghissue{931}.}

\section{The formal statement}

The Lean development provides the corresponding theorem but does not
include the $\simeq_{\nu}$ consistency hypothesis \eqref{eq:paper-nu-consistency}.%
\footnote{The Lean declaration is \leanid{MIPStarRE.LDT.SelfImprovement.selfImprovement}
in \doclink{MIPStarRE/LDT/SelfImprovement/Theorems/Results.lean}, lines~172--262.}
Instead it takes three explicit bridge-input hypotheses:\footnote{The
bridge-input structure is \leanid{SelfImprovementBridgeInputs}
in \doclink{MIPStarRE/LDT/SelfImprovement/Theorems/Statements.lean},
lines~363--378.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item a strong-self-consistency input for the averaged helper measurement,
  \item an orthonormalization input, and
  \item a final-fields input packaging the remaining conclusions.
\end{itemize}
The parameter $\nu$ appears in the theorem's conclusion---the completeness
bound is $(1-\nu)-$\emph{selfImprovementError}---but there is no hypothesis
tying $\nu$ to the input measurement $G$. The argument $G$ is passed to the
helper lemma as an explicit unused parameter.

In the induction-step wrapper%
\footnote{\leanid{MIPStarRE.LDT.MainInductionStep.selfImprovementInInductionSection}
in \doclink{MIPStarRE/LDT/MainInductionStep/Theorems.lean}, lines~66--155.}
the $\simeq_{\nu}$ hypothesis does appear as a formal parameter, but the
proof immediately delegates to a bridge-input-based theorem that ignores it.
The hypothesis is thus vestigial at both the standalone and the
induction-section level.

\section{Mathematical consequence}

The omission of $\simeq_{\nu}$ weakens the mathematical content of the
formal theorem. In the type of the Lean theorem, $\nu$ is a free real
parameter with no constraint beyond its appearance in the error expression
$(1-\nu)-\mathrm{error}$. Because the Lean theorem's hypotheses make no
demands on $\nu$ and do not relate $\nu$ to any property of $G$, a call
site is free to supply $\nu=1$; the bound then reads $-\mathrm{error}$,
which is mathematically trivial. More generally, the theorem does not
construct a small $\nu$ from $G$ and $A$; it merely accepts an arbitrary
$\nu$ and promises that \emph{if} the bridge-input hypotheses are supplied,
the conclusions follow. This is a genuine logical gap between the paper
statement and the formal statement, not a notational convention. The bridge
inputs record the missing proof obligations explicitly, but they do not
restore a hypothesis on $G$ to the statement.

\section{Blueprint status}

The blueprint entry for the self-improvement theorem%
\footnote{\path{blueprint/src/chapter/ch07_self_improvement.tex},
lines~490--495.}
lists the four Lean declarations and marks them \leanid{\textbackslash leanok}.
While all four compile without \leanid{sorry}, the blueprint does not
make the hypothesis discrepancy apparent. The Lean statement and the paper
statement differ, and the blueprint's \leanid{\textbackslash leanok}
annotation could be read as asserting that the full paper theorem is
formalized without additional hypotheses.

\section{Error coefficients}

All error constants match the paper when the bridge inputs are discharged.
The helper error $100\,m\,(\eps^{1/2}+\delta^{1/2}+(d/q)^{1/2})$ matches
the paper's $\widehat\zeta$; the orthogonalization error delegates to the
orthonormalization infrastructure, which uses the paper's $100\widehat\zeta^{\,1/4}$;
the data-processing error $8\widehat\zeta+8\sqrt{\widehat\zeta_{\text{ortho}}}$
matches the paper; and the final self-improvement error
$3000\,m\,(\eps^{1/32}+\delta^{1/32}+(d/q)^{1/32})$ matches the paper's $\zeta$.

\section{Conclusion}

The paper's self-improvement theorem requires an approximation hypothesis
$\simeq_{\nu}$ linking the input measurement $G$ to the parent measurement
$A$. The formal theorem omits this hypothesis and instead accepts a
package of bridge inputs. Until the bridge inputs are discharged in a way
that recovers the missing $\simeq_{\nu}$ constraint on $G$, or the
blueprint is amended to document the changed statement, the formal theorem
does not match the paper's self-improvement theorem.
