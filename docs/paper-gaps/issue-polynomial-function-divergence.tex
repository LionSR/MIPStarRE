\section{The paper's definition}

In \cite{Ji2020LowIndividualDegree}, an $m$-variate polynomial over
$\mathbb{F}_q$ is defined as a \emph{function}
$g:\mathbb{F}_q^m \to \mathbb{F}_q$ of the form
\[
  g(x_1,\dots,x_m) = \sum_{i_1,\dots,i_m}
    c_{i_1,\dots,i_m} \cdot x_1^{i_1}\cdots x_m^{i_m},
\]
with coefficients $c_{i_1,\dots,i_m} \in
\mathbb{F}_q$.\footnote{See \path{references/ldt-paper/introduction.tex},
line~3.}
Individual degree $d$ means $i_1,\dots,i_m \le d$ for every nonzero
coefficient. The set
\[
  \polyfunc{m}{q}{d}
  \subset \{ \mathbb{F}_q^m \to \mathbb{F}_q \}
\]
is then the set of functions that admit such a
representation.\footnote{See
\path{references/ldt-paper/preliminaries.tex}, lines~92--93.}
This is the standard convention in theoretical computer science:
``polynomial'' means a function that can be written as an algebraic
polynomial.

\section{The formal definition}

The formalization defines a low-individual-degree
predicate\footnote{The declaration is named
\texttt{HasLowIndividualDegree} and appears in
\path{MIPStarRE/LDT/Basic/LinePolynomials.lean}, line~20.} on a
function $g : \mathbb{F}_q^m \to \mathbb{F}_q$ as follows:
\[
  \operatorname{LowDeg}_d(g) \;:=\;
  \exists\, p \in \mathbb{F}_q[x_1,\dots,x_m],\;
    \bigl(\forall i,\ \deg_{x_i}(p) \le d\bigr)
    \;\wedge\;
    g = \operatorname{eval}(p).
\]
Here $p$ ranges over the algebraic polynomial ring over the chosen
finite field,\footnote{This is the abbreviation
\texttt{PolynomialModel}, which expands to
\texttt{MvPolynomial (Fin m) (Scalar params)} in
\path{MIPStarRE/LDT/Basic/ParametersBase.lean}, line~300.}
and $\operatorname{eval}$ is its coefficient-wise evaluation map to a
function $\mathbb{F}_q^m \to \mathbb{F}_q$. Thus the predicate asserts
exactly the paper's condition: the function $g$ admits an algebraic
polynomial representative of the right degree.

The earlier blueprint used a function-type representation for
$\polyfunc{m}{q}{d}$, treating low-degree polynomials as a subtype of
the function space. That representation has been corrected to the
$\exists$ predicate above, which matches the paper's definition
exactly.\footnote{The blueprint correction is visible in
\path{blueprint/src/chapter/ch02_test.tex} and
\path{blueprint/src/chapter/ch03_preliminaries.tex}, where
$\polyfunc{m}{q}{d}$ is now the set of functions representable as an
algebraic polynomial of individual degree $\le d$.}

\section{Why algebraic polynomials?}

The paper's proofs (and the formalization) require algebraic
polynomials for the Schwartz--Zippel lemma.\footnote{Labelled
\texttt{lem:schwartz-zippel-total-degree} in the paper, and available
in Mathlib as \texttt{MvPolynomial.schwartzZippel}.}
The Mathlib statement operates on algebraic polynomials, not on
arbitrary functions. The low-individual-degree predicate is therefore
the natural bridge: it takes a function~$g$ and extracts the algebraic
polynomial~$p$ that witnesses its low individual degree, enabling the
application of Schwartz--Zippel.

The measurement families $G$, $A$, $B$, $L$ in the formalization carry
outcomes indexed by \emph{polynomials}, and the evaluation maps
$g \mapsto G_{g}$ respect the algebraic polynomial structure.
This design closely follows the paper, where measurement outcomes are
indexed by $g \in \polyfunc{m}{q}{d}$ and the Schwartz--Zippel step
uses algebraic properties of the polynomials.

\section{Conclusion}

\noindent\textbf{Verdict:} no divergence. The paper defines polynomials
as functions with a polynomial representative; the formalization
defines the low-individual-degree predicate as the existence of an
algebraic polynomial representative. The two are mathematically
equivalent. The earlier blueprint, which used function types directly,
has been corrected to the $\exists$ predicate, eliminating the only
discrepancy.
